  • Beliefs And Morality

    • By Theophilus Nuntaa Nabile on May 15, 2014
    • 5 comments
    • Bootstrap Religion

    Religion has lost its nominal essence in recent times and thus people have resorted to having a belief or a relationship with a higher deity hence the choice of word belief. Morality is a universal certainty regardless of an individual’s beliefs, however, the paradigm by which morality is described is subjective to a person’s belief(s). Morality must have a standard to discern right from wrong. Let us take a court house, a case is brought before the house, a jury decides whether the defendant is acquitted or convicted. In applying this analogy, the court house is a person’s belief, the case is morality, the jury is the standard, and acquittal and conviction stands for right and wrong respectively. Based on different juries, what one jury might choose right would be wrong to another. This is the problem we face with morality; who is right, who is wrong and who is more right? That phrase always puzzles me, more right? How much right can one be? My focus is not on morality between a person and the divine being, but morality amongst humans, therefore atheists and agnostics are included.

    Subjectivity in morality is influenced by the diverse principles instigated by different beliefs. The Christians believe in God, and God’s words are their standard. The Muslims believe in Allah, and Allah’s words are their standard. The atheists also have their gods, “logic, common sense and science”. So long as behavior is based on belief or vice versa, compromising and tolerance become challenging. Co-existence is easier when there is a common ground. So how is a common ground for morality amongst humans attainable when the standards are different? There exists a common standard, a standard older than all current beliefs thus morality that existed before all existing beliefs.
